On the missing data layer and a potential solution

a regional datahub

Marian Basti† · Mauro Ibañez† · Kenn Bro† · Francis F. Daniel† · Francis Perelman‡
†SURUS Team  ·  ‡Independent
18 June 2026 · Buenos Aires
Abstract

One of the foundational layers for AI in Latin America is missing: the dataset layer. Without it, the region cannot train its own models — and the bill for that absence lands in two lanes the region already tracks. On the social side, public institutions procure models trained on foreign curricula, foreign correspondence conventions, and foreign value systems; the failures show up downstream in literacy, health outcomes, judicial efficiency, and citizen access — the substrate of the Human Development Index. On the economic side, a pest classifier trained on European agriculture mislabels Argentine harvests; a legal summarizer trained on US case law misses an Argentine civil filing; a medical NER model trained on English clinical notes fails on a Mexican EHR — silent miscalls on the exact tasks regional companies need AI to perform, compounding harvest by harvest, filing by filing, into GDP forgone.

The dataset layer faces two compounding problems. Discovery: LATAM datasets exist — across Hugging Face, Common Voice, the Internet Archive, Chinese data hubs, paper appendices, and internal institutional archives — but no shared index points to them. Supply: even pooled, the total sits far below what the US, China, and Europe accumulated through years of grant requirements, open-data mandates, and academic norms that pushed institutions to publish. The two reinforce: with nowhere to publish for return, fewer datasets get released; with few releases, no critical mass forms. The Data Hub breaks the loop from both ends — indexing what exists, and making contribution a visible, rewarded act.

The Hub is organized around a task-first ontology: task first (transcription, classification, translation), then domain (medical, legal, broadcast), then language and regional variant — Rioplatense Spanish is not interchangeable with Castilian, nor Brazilian Portuguese with European; indigenous languages enter as first-class entries, not long-tail afterthoughts behind a generic “low-resource” filter. The walk from general to specific mirrors how a practitioner actually searches.

We argue for a multipolar AI world with Latin America as one pole, contributing on its own terms. The Hub is therefore open by design — a region without incumbent hyperscalers moves faster through a commons than through silos — and incentive-driven by construction, since a commons stays alive only when contributors are rewarded: each dataset compounds the value of every prior one, and the contributing-organizations carousel converts participation into institutional recognition. Open questions remain: regulatory heterogeneity across jurisdictions, shared standards for metadata and licensing, collaboration norms (attribution, versioning, arbitration, review), and the institutional incentives needed for universities and governments to publish data they currently keep internal. This paper is a kickstart, not a finished proposal — an invitation to researchers, institutions, and companies across the region to shape what the dataset layer for LATAM AI should become.

01 Introduction

The state of AI in Latin America today is one of consumption. The region uses models built elsewhere, trained on data collected elsewhere, evaluated against tasks defined elsewhere. Consumption itself is not the problem — many AI capabilities are universal. The problem is that today the region cannot do anything else: of the foundational layers AI requires, one — the dataset layer — is missing. Without datasets to train on, no regional model gets built, and the foreign models the region already runs go unchallenged.

This paper introduces the Data Hub, the Surus team's attempt at that dataset layer — a working artifact, not a finished proposal. The cost of the missing layer is concrete and measurable, on indicators the region already tracks (§2). The existing landscape is broken in specific, addressable ways (§3). The right ontology for a regional dataset layer is task-first, not modality-first (§4). The Hub must be open by design and incentive-driven by construction (§5). Real problems remain open (§6), and the work cannot be done by one team (§7).

02 Two Lanes of Cost: GDP and Human Development

The cost of the missing dataset layer is not abstract. It lands in two lanes the region can already feel, in indicators the region already tracks.

2.1 The Social Lane — Public Institutions and the Human Development Index

Public institutions — ministries, schools, hospitals, courts — increasingly procure language models, classifiers, and decision-support tools. Each arrives pre-trained on a corpus reflecting the conventions, biases, and curricula of where it was assembled. A language model used for citizen correspondence carries the register of another country's bureaucracy. An educational chatbot's notion of a good answer was shaped by curricula developed elsewhere. A triage model deployed in a public hospital encodes a different epidemiological prior than the population it serves actually lives under. The failures are not malicious — they are silent, and they accumulate in exactly the indicators that summarize a region's social performance: literacy, health outcomes, access to public services, judicial efficiency — the substrate of the Human Development Index. A region that staffs its public-service backbone with foreign-trained AI lets foreign value systems write its HDI trajectory.

2.2 The Economic Lane — Companies and GDP

The same failure mode lands inside industry, where it shows up directly in output. A pest-classification model trained on European or North American imagery will not reliably identify the species destroying a Colombian coffee crop, a southern-cone wheat field, or a Brazilian soybean harvest. A legal-summarization model trained on US case law misses the procedural structure of an Argentine civil filing. A medical NER model trained on English clinical notes does not recognize the terminology in a Mexican electronic health record. These are silent miscalls on the exact tasks the regional economy needs AI to perform — and the cost compounds, harvest by harvest, filing by filing, claim by claim, into GDP forgone. As AI moves from optional capability to embedded tooling, the gap between “works for the region” and “works for somewhere else” stops being a research curiosity and becomes a production-line tax.

2.3 The Lanes Are Interlocked

The two lanes are not independent. Weak institutions slow the economy; an underperforming economy starves institutions. A dataset layer that lets the region train models on its own languages, its own agriculture, its own jurisprudence, and its own clinical practice is the prerequisite for moving both indicators in the right direction at once. Without it, the region pays the cost on both sides simultaneously, and the bill compounds with every additional AI system procured.

03 The State of LATAM Datasets: Discovery and Supply

The dataset layer faces two compounding problems. They are usually discussed separately, but they reinforce each other and must be addressed together.

3.1 Discovery

LATAM datasets exist. The problem is that they sit across at least six kinds of locations, with no shared index:

  • General-purpose hubs like Hugging Face host them, buried under foreign-language defaults and modality-first organization that assumes the practitioner already knows what they want.
  • Foundational web archives such as Mozilla's Common Voice hold region-specific speech, but a researcher looking for Rioplatense transcription cannot navigate from “I need this” to “this is where it lives” without already knowing the project name.
  • The Internet Archive holds digitized regional materials of significant linguistic value, but discoverability runs on full-text search, not task affordance.
  • Chinese data hubs have become an important locus for multilingual datasets — several LATAM-relevant corpora live there — but linguistic and platform friction keep them invisible to most regional practitioners.
  • Academic paper appendices are perhaps the largest single source: built by graduate students at regional universities, used once for a paper's experiments, then abandoned to the archival half-life of a PDF.
  • Internal institutional archives — government records, medical archives, court documents, university research — hold significant volumes that have never been published as datasets at all.

A practitioner asking “which datasets train a model for medical transcription in Argentinian Spanish?” can answer only by personal network, accident, or weeks of search. This is not a failure of any platform; it is the absence of a regional index.

3.2 Supply

Even with perfect indexing tomorrow, the total volume would remain far below what the US, China, and Europe accumulated — not because LATAM researchers are less capable, but because those regions spent years actively pushing institutions, companies, and researchers to publish through grant requirements, open-data initiatives, corporate programs, and academic norms. LATAM has not. The deficit compounds annually: every year without an active creation effort widens the gap.

3.3 The Reinforcing Loop

Discovery and supply are not independent. There is nowhere to publish a LATAM dataset where the contributor sees a return on the act. A researcher who spends months curating a Rioplatense legal corpus has nowhere both visible to the regional AI community and attributable in a way that benefits a career or institution; the dataset gets dropped into a global hub with default settings, indistinguishable from thousands of others. So fewer datasets get published — and because few are published, no flywheel of “I see other organizations contributing, so my contribution will be seen alongside theirs” ever forms. The loop closes. The Data Hub breaks it from both ends: indexing what exists through a LATAM-first ontology (§4), and making contribution a visible, rewarded act (§5).

04 A Task-First Ontology

Existing dataset catalogs almost universally organize by input modality (text, image, audio, video) or by target model. Both are wrong for a practitioner, who does not start from “I have an image” but from “I need a system that does Y.” The relevant taxonomy is organized around what an AI system does — its task — and refines by domain and language. The Hub adopts a task-first ontology with three nested levels.

4.1 Task

An AI model is fundamentally a program for a task: transcription, classification, translation, summarization, question answering, information extraction, generation, retrieval. This is the first axis because no further refinement matters until the task is fixed — a medical transcription dataset is useless to someone building a legal classifier. Tasks are defined at the level of what work the system performs, not model architecture; one dataset may support several.

4.2 Domain

Within a task, the second axis is domain. A transcription dataset becomes useful when it is specifically medical, legal, broadcast journalism, or educational. Vocabulary, acoustic conditions, and annotation conventions all differ; a model trained on medical transcription fails unevenly on legal audio, even within the same language. The practitioner asking “is there a transcription dataset I can use?” should immediately be asked “what domain?” — because that determines whether any candidate is usable.

4.3 Language and Regional Variant

The third axis is language — and within language, regional variant. This is where LATAM-first design departs most sharply from global hubs, which treat Spanish as a single tag and Portuguese as another. Medical transcription in Rioplatense Spanish is not interchangeable with Castilian: clinical vocabulary, patient-facing register, and acoustic patterns all differ — and a model trained on one fails on the other, silently. The same holds for Brazilian versus European Portuguese, Mexican versus Chilean Spanish, and across indigenous languages where “variant” requires careful definition. The Hub's language ontology is LATAM-first by construction: Spanish decomposed by regional variant, Portuguese by Brazilian versus European, indigenous languages as first-class entries — not long-tail afterthoughts behind a generic “low-resource” filter.

4.4 The Hierarchy in Practice

A practitioner walks the hierarchy from general to specific: transcription → medical → Rioplatense Spanish. Each step narrows the candidate set. The same axes guide a contributor: declaring task, domain, and variant places a new dataset in the exact slot where someone searching for it will look. The ontology is the index and the publishing form in one.

05 Open by Design, Incentive-Driven by Construction

The Hub's design follows from a position about what kind of AI world we want, and what role Latin America should play in it.

5.1 A Multipolar AI World, with LATAM as One Pole

We argue for a multipolar AI world — one in which AI is developed across many regions, by many communities, reflecting many sets of values — and we believe Latin America should be one of those poles. AI built in many places by many communities is more robust, more representative, and more aligned with humanity's actual diversity than AI built in a handful of places by a handful of organizations. This is not an argument against collaboration; it is an argument for LATAM contributing on its own terms. A region that builds its own dataset layer collaborates from capacity; one that depends entirely on foreign infrastructure can only collaborate from dependence.

5.2 Why Open

Different regions have taken different routes. Some have built largely closed and proprietary ecosystems, with high-value datasets and models held inside a small number of large organizations; others have built open and collaborative ones. Both can work — but we believe open is right for LATAM, for two reasons. First, a region without incumbent hyperscalers moves faster through a shared commons than through closed silos. The closed model works when a few organizations already hold most of the capital, talent, and data; their internal accumulation outpaces external collaboration. LATAM is not in that position. What it has is many capable researchers, institutions with relevant data, and companies with regional expertise — distributed across the region. The fastest way to assemble these is to pool them in a commons, not silo them inside organizations that cannot reach critical mass alone. Second, open invites contribution; closed forbids it. The Hub's value comes from network effects: the more datasets it indexes, the more useful it is; the more practitioners use it, the more contributors are motivated. None of this flywheel exists in a closed-by-default model.

5.3 Why Incentive-Driven

A commons is not self-sustaining by virtue of being a commons; without explicit incentives it becomes a tragedy in reverse — nobody publishes, because nobody else is publishing. Each new dataset compounds the value of every prior contribution: the index grows more complete, the ontology more populated; early contributors make later ones worth making. Visibility is the reward. A contributor — university lab, public institution, private company, independent researcher — gains something concrete from publishing: visibility within the regional AI community, attribution attached to their dataset, brand recognition for their organization. The contributing-organizations carousel is one concrete mechanism: contributing organizations are surfaced on the Hub itself, converting the act of contribution into ongoing institutional recognition. For public institutions especially — universities, ministries, research agencies — that visibility is a meaningful return otherwise difficult to capture. These mechanisms are first-pass; as the Hub grows, additional structures will be needed for sustained contribution, quality, and community-led curation. We treat the incentive layer as something to evolve, not finalize.

5.4 A Continuum, Not a Catalog

The Hub is a continuum, not a static catalog. A catalog goes stale from the moment it is published; a continuum is a living system with active contribution, curation, and growth. The consequences are structural: the ontology must be extensible — new tasks, domains, and variants addable without breaking existing entries; versioning must be first-class; the contribution path must be low-friction, so a researcher who has just produced a dataset publishes in minutes, not weeks; and the social layer — the relationships among contributors, users, and curators — must be visible and supported, not buried under interface friction.

06 Open Problems

The Hub does not resolve every question implied by the dataset layer it begins to build. Naming the open ones is more useful than papering over them.

6.1 Regulatory Heterogeneity

Data protection, copyright, and public-data access regimes differ country by country. A dataset assembled from public health records may be uncomplicated to publish in one country and legally fraught in a neighbor. Frameworks around scraped web data, government open-data mandates, indigenous-language data sovereignty, and personal-data protection do not align across borders. The Hub cannot resolve these — they are matters of national law — but it must surface the licensing and legal-jurisdiction information of every dataset it indexes, so practitioners decide with the relevant context visible.

6.2 Shared Standards

There are no shared standards for dataset metadata, licensing, or quality in LATAM. Two datasets nominally about “Spanish transcription” may use incompatible annotation schemas, incompatible licenses, and incompatible quality bars. Without shared standards, datasets cannot be combined or compared. We do not propose one here — that work is too large for a kickstart — but we name the gap and treat the Hub's initial metadata schema as a first proposal to be debated, not a final answer.

6.3 Collaboration Norms

Separate from metadata standards, the Hub raises governance questions not yet answered:

  • Attribution. When organization A contributes a dataset and organization B improves it (cleans it, adds annotations, fixes errors), how is credit allocated and represented?
  • Versioning. As datasets evolve, how do downstream users — including model trainers who have already trained on prior versions — track which version they are working with?
  • Arbitration. When a community contests how a dataset represents it — an indigenous community objecting to how their language has been transcribed and tagged — how is that objection processed, and what authority resolves it?
  • Review. What review process gates entry: open by default with post-hoc moderation, curated pre-publication review, or a hybrid?
  • License inheritance. When datasets are combined, how do their licenses propagate, and how is this enforced or merely tracked?

The Hub has initial answers; none should be treated as final. These are questions for the regional community to decide together.

6.4 Institutional Incentives

Universities, governments, research agencies, and public bodies hold significant volumes of data valuable as published datasets. Most never gets released, for a mix of reasons: no career incentive, no institutional mandate, no clear pathway, no budget for the cleanup. The Hub's visibility mechanisms address part of this; the deeper problem requires policy work outside the Hub — grant requirements that mandate dataset publication, university promotion criteria that value dataset contributions alongside papers, government open-data policies that treat AI-readable formats as a first-class deliverable. These are for the regional academic and policy communities. The Hub is a precondition for those conversations, not a substitute.

07 A Kickstart and an Invitation

This paper is a kickstart — a working artifact and a position, not a finished proposal. The Surus team has built the first version of the Data Hub because somebody had to begin, and the dataset layer for LATAM AI is too important to wait for. But the Hub does not belong to us, and it cannot be built by us alone.

We invite three kinds of contribution.

To researchers and institutions holding data: publish into the Hub. Whether your dataset is the appendix of a paper from three years ago, the working corpus of an active project, or the internal archive of an institution that has never published anything — the Hub is built to receive it. Every marginal contribution increases the value of every prior one.

To AI practitioners across the region: use the Hub. The most important signal that it matters is that practitioners are choosing datasets from it, building models against it, and evaluating systems through it. Use is what converts a commons from a directory into infrastructure.

To the broader community — universities, foundations, governments, companies: help shape what comes next. The ontology will evolve, the standards must be debated, the collaboration norms must be defined. None of this can be the work of a single team.

The dataset layer for Latin American AI will be built by the region, for the region, or it will not be built at all — and with it, the sovereign capacity to build, evaluate, and steer the technology that is becoming the region's infrastructure. The Data Hub is one step toward that future. We invite you to take the next one with us.
