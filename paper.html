On the missing data layer and a potential solution

datahub.lat

Marian Basti† · Mauro Ibañez† · Kenn Bro† · Francis F. Daniel† · Francis Perelman‡
†SURUS Team  ·  ‡Independent
18 June 2026 · Buenos Aires
Abstract

Latin America is missing two foundational layers for AI: a dataset layer and a benchmark layer. The cost is dual. On the public-institution side, models trained elsewhere encode foreign value systems, languages, and historical narratives — shaping how citizens are represented, served, and governed. On the industry side, the same models are optimized for foreign tasks and fail silently on regional ones: a pest-classification model trained on European or North American agriculture will not reliably identify the pests that determine harvests in Argentina or Brazil. The result is a loss of sovereign capacity over a technology that is becoming infrastructure.

These layers are the minimum prerequisites for building and judging AI. Without datasets we cannot train our own models. Without benchmarks we cannot evaluate them — nor evaluate the foreign systems we are already using. If no benchmark exists for Latin American historical knowledge, there is no way to know whether GPT-5 or DeepSeek represent the region accurately. Institutions need this for procurement; industries to avoid silent underperformance; the region to train AI that reflects its own languages, contexts, and values.

The dataset layer faces two compounding problems. Discovery: LATAM datasets exist — on Hugging Face, in the Mozilla Foundation, in the Internet Archive, in Chinese data hubs, in the appendices of university papers — but no shared index points to them. Supply: even pooled, the volume sits far below what the United States, China, and Europe have accumulated, because those regions spent years actively pushing institutions and researchers to publish into shared hubs. The two reinforce each other: with nowhere to publish for return, fewer datasets get released; with few releases, no critical mass forms. The Data Hub breaks the loop from both ends — indexing what already exists, and making contribution a visible, rewarded act.

The Hub is organized around a task-first ontology: three nested levels mirroring how a practitioner searches. Task comes first — an AI model is fundamentally a program for a task: transcription, classification, translation, summarization. Domain comes second: a transcription dataset becomes useful when it is specifically medical or legal. Language comes third, sub-typed by regional variant: medical transcription in Rioplatense Spanish is not interchangeable with Castilian Spanish or Brazilian Portuguese. The ontology is designed so the walk from general to specific is the natural one.

We argue for a multipolar AI world and believe Latin America should be one of its poles, with its own technology and its own agency. From that position the Hub must be open by design and incentive-driven by construction: a region without incumbent hyperscalers moves faster through a shared commons than through closed silos, and a commons stays alive only when contributors are rewarded. Each new dataset compounds the value of every prior one; mechanisms like the contributing-organizations carousel convert participation into institutional recognition. Open questions remain — regulatory heterogeneity across LATAM jurisdictions, shared standards for metadata and licensing, collaboration norms (attribution, versioning, arbitration, review), and the institutional incentives needed for universities and governments to publish data they currently keep internal. This paper is a kickstart by the Surus team — a working artifact and a position — and an invitation to researchers, institutions, and companies across the region to help shape what the dataset layer for LATAM AI should become.

01 Introduction

The state of AI in Latin America today is one of consumption. The region uses models built elsewhere, trained on data collected elsewhere, evaluated against tasks defined elsewhere. Consumption is not, by itself, the problem — many AI capabilities are universal. The problem is that the region today cannot do anything else. It cannot build its own AI, because it lacks the dataset layer to train models. It cannot evaluate AI, because it lacks the benchmark layer to judge whether any model — foreign or domestic — actually performs against the tasks the region cares about.

This paper introduces the Data Hub, an open initiative by the Surus team to build the dataset layer for LATAM AI. We describe what is broken about the current landscape, why existing taxonomies are wrong, what ontology should replace them, why the Hub must be open and incentive-driven by construction, and what problems remain open. The benchmark layer is an equally necessary parallel project, addressed in companion work; a region that has built only one of the two is still structurally dependent.

On the public-institution side, models trained elsewhere encode foreign value systems, languages, and historical narratives. When a public agency procures a language model for citizen correspondence, the model arrives pre-trained on a corpus reflecting the conventions and biases of where it was assembled. When a school district evaluates an educational chatbot, that chatbot's notion of a good answer was shaped by curricula developed elsewhere. The failures are not malicious — they are silent. The model works; it is simply optimized for a different set of citizens and institutions than the ones using it.

On the industry side, the failure mode is structurally identical. A pest-classification model trained on European or North American imagery will not reliably identify the species destroying a Colombian coffee crop, a southern-cone wheat field, or a Brazilian soybean harvest. A legal-summarization model trained on US case law will miss the procedural structure of an Argentine civil filing. A medical NER model trained on English clinical notes will not recognize the terminology in a Mexican electronic health record. These are silent miscalls on the exact tasks the regional economy needs AI to perform.

The sum is a loss of sovereign capacity over a technology that is becoming infrastructure — the layer through which governments make decisions, citizens access information, and industries operate. A region without its own dataset and benchmark layers cannot build, evaluate, or steer that layer. The Data Hub exists to begin closing that gap.

02 Why Both Layers Are Prerequisites: Training and Evaluation

Datasets and benchmarks are not optional infrastructure. They are the minimum prerequisites for both building AI systems and judging them.

Without datasets we cannot train. Every model, agent, and downstream system depends on data — foundation models trained from scratch, fine-tuned task-specific systems, retrieval-augmented systems grounded in a corpus, agents learning from interaction logs. The hyperscalers did not arrive at dominance by clever architecture alone; they arrived by spending years assembling, cleaning, and structuring corpora at unprecedented scale. Whatever LATAM eventually builds — sovereign foundation models or task-specialized systems on top of foreign bases — that work cannot begin without a usable dataset substrate.

Without benchmarks we cannot evaluate, and they serve two constituencies. Anyone building AI for LATAM — researchers, startups, large companies — needs to know whether what they have built actually works. Without regional benchmarks, development proceeds blind: developers either use foreign benchmarks that measure the wrong tasks, or build private internal evaluations that produce no shared regional knowledge. Anyone procuring or deploying foreign AI in LATAM — public institutions, government agencies, large companies — needs to know whether a foreign model is fit for purpose. If no benchmark exists for Latin American historical knowledge, there is no way for a ministry of education to determine whether GPT-5 or DeepSeek represent the region's history accurately enough to be used in a state curriculum. The decision then defaults to whatever the vendor's marketing claims — a poor substitute for measurement.

The Data Hub focuses on the dataset layer, but the two layers are interdependent: a benchmark is a labeled dataset evaluated against a model, and the same collection efforts often produce both. Naming them as distinct layers is useful because they answer distinct questions — datasets answer what can we train on?, benchmarks answer how do we know if it works? — and a region that has built only one is still structurally dependent.

03 The State of LATAM Datasets: Discovery and Supply

The dataset layer faces two compounding problems. They are usually discussed separately, but they reinforce each other and must be addressed together.

3.1 Discovery

LATAM datasets do exist. The problem is that they sit across at least six kinds of locations, with no shared index pointing to them:

  • General-purpose hubs like Hugging Face host LATAM datasets, but they are buried under foreign-language defaults, and organization-by-modality assumes a practitioner already knows what they want.
  • Foundational web archives such as Mozilla's Common Voice contain region-specific speech data, but a researcher looking for Rioplatense Spanish transcription cannot navigate from “I need this” to “this is where it lives” without already knowing the project name.
  • The Internet Archive holds digitized regional materials of significant linguistic value, but discoverability runs on full-text search, not AI-task affordance.
  • Chinese data hubs have become an important locus for multilingual datasets, and several LATAM-relevant corpora live there — but linguistic and platform friction keep them invisible to most regional practitioners.
  • The appendices of academic papers are perhaps the largest single source of LATAM datasets — built by graduate students at regional universities, used once for a paper's experiments, then abandoned to the archival half-life of a PDF.
  • Internal institutional archives — government records, medical archives, court documents, university research — hold significant volumes of LATAM-relevant data that has never been published as datasets at all.

A practitioner asking “which datasets can I use to train a model for medical transcription in Argentinian Spanish?” has no way to answer except by personal network, accident, or weeks of search. This is not a failure of any individual platform; it is the absence of a regional index.

3.2 Supply

Even if every existing LATAM dataset were perfectly indexed tomorrow, the total volume would remain substantially below what the United States, China, and Europe have accumulated — not because LATAM researchers are less capable, but because those regions have spent years actively pushing institutions, companies, and researchers to publish into shared hubs through grant requirements, government data initiatives, corporate open-data programs, and academic norms. LATAM has not. The deficit compounds annually: every year without an active dataset-creation effort is a year the gap widens.

3.3 The Reinforcing Loop

Discovery and supply are not independent. There is no place to publish a LATAM dataset where the contributor sees a return on the act of contributing. A researcher who spends months curating a Rioplatense Spanish legal corpus has nowhere to publish it that is both visible to the regional AI community and attributable to the contributor in a way that benefits their career or institution. The dataset gets dropped into a global hub with default settings, indistinguishable from thousands of others. So fewer datasets get published — and because few are published, no critical mass forms, no flywheel of “I see other organizations contributing, so my contribution will be seen alongside theirs.” The loop closes.

The Data Hub breaks it from both ends. On the discovery side, it indexes what already exists through a single LATAM-first ontology. On the supply side, it makes contribution a visible, rewarded act — through mechanisms detailed in Section 5.

04 A Task-First Ontology

Existing dataset catalogs almost universally organize entries by input modality (text, image, audio, video) or by target model. Both are wrong for a practitioner, who does not start from “I have an image” or “I want to use model X” but from “I need to build a system that does Y.” The relevant taxonomy is organized around what an AI system does — its task — and progressively refines by domain and language. The Hub adopts a task-first ontology with three nested levels.

4.1 Task

An AI model is fundamentally a program for a task — transcription, classification, translation, summarization, question answering, information extraction, generation, retrieval. This is the first axis, because no further refinement matters until the task is fixed: a medical transcription dataset is useless to someone building a legal classifier. In the Hub's registry, tasks are defined at the level of what work the system performs, not at the level of model architecture; one dataset may support several.

4.2 Domain

Within a task, the second axis is domain. A transcription dataset becomes useful when it is specifically medical, legal, broadcast journalism, or educational transcription. Vocabulary differs. Acoustic conditions differ. Annotation conventions differ. A model trained on medical transcription will produce uneven results on legal audio, even within the same language. A practitioner asking “is there a transcription dataset I can use?” should be guided immediately to “what domain do you need?” — because that determines whether any candidate is actually usable.

4.3 Language and Regional Variant

The third axis is language — and within language, regional variant. This is where LATAM-first design departs most sharply from global hubs, which typically treat Spanish as a single tag and Portuguese as another. Medical transcription in Rioplatense Spanish is not interchangeable with medical transcription in Castilian Spanish. Clinical vocabulary differs, patient-facing register differs, acoustic patterns differ — and a model trained on one will fail on the other, silently. The same holds for Brazilian versus European Portuguese, Mexican versus Chilean Spanish, and across indigenous LATAM languages where the notion of “variant” requires careful definition.

The Hub's language ontology is LATAM-first by construction: Spanish decomposed by regional variant, Portuguese by Brazilian versus European, indigenous LATAM languages as first-class entries — not long-tail afterthoughts behind a generic “low-resource” filter.

4.4 The Hierarchy in Practice

A practitioner walks the hierarchy from general to specific: task, then domain, then language and variant. I need a transcription dataset → for medical use → in Rioplatense Spanish. Each step narrows the candidate set. The benchmark layer, when built, will inherit the same structure: a benchmark for medical transcription in Rioplatense Spanish is the natural evaluation companion to a dataset described at the same specificity. This parallelism is intentional.

05 Open by Design, Incentive-Driven by Construction

The Hub's design follows from a position about what kind of AI world we want to live in, and what role Latin America should play in it.

5.1 A Multipolar AI World, with LATAM as One Pole

We argue for a multipolar AI world — one in which AI is developed across many regions, by many communities, reflecting many sets of values — and we believe Latin America should be one of those poles. AI built in many places by many communities is more robust, more representative, and more aligned with humanity's actual diversity than AI built in a handful of places by a handful of organizations. This is not an argument against collaboration; it is an argument for LATAM contributing on its own terms — as a contributor, not only as a consumer. A region that builds its own dataset and benchmark layers can collaborate from a position of capacity; one that depends entirely on foreign infrastructure can only collaborate from dependence.

5.2 Why Open

Different regions have taken different routes. Some have built AI ecosystems that are largely closed and proprietary, with high-value datasets and models held inside a small number of large organizations. Others have built ecosystems that are open and collaborative. Both routes can work — but we believe open is the right one for LATAM, for two reasons.

First, a region without incumbent hyperscalers moves faster through a shared commons than through closed silos. The closed model works when a few large organizations already hold most of the capital, talent, and data; their internal accumulation outpaces any external collaboration. LATAM is not in that position. What it does have is many capable researchers, many institutions with relevant data, and many companies with regional expertise — distributed across the region. The fastest way to assemble these into AI capacity is to pool them in a commons, not silo them inside organizations that cannot reach critical mass alone.

Second, open invites contribution; closed forbids it. The Hub's value comes from network effects: the more datasets it indexes, the more useful it is; the more practitioners use it, the more contributors are motivated. None of this flywheel exists in a closed-by-default model.

5.3 Why Incentive-Driven

A commons is not self-sustaining by virtue of being a commons. One that nobody owns and everyone owns stays alive only when contributors are rewarded. Without explicit incentives, the commons becomes a tragedy in reverse: nobody publishes, because nobody else is publishing.

Compounding value. Each new dataset compounds the value of every prior contribution: the index becomes more complete, the ontology more populated. Early contributors are the ones who make later contributions worth making.

Visibility as reward. A contributor — university lab, public institution, private company, independent researcher — gains something concrete from publishing: visibility within the regional AI community, attribution attached to their dataset, brand recognition for their organization. The contributing-organizations carousel is one concrete mechanism: contributing organizations are surfaced on the Hub itself, converting the act of contribution into ongoing institutional recognition. For public institutions especially — universities, ministries, research agencies — that visibility is a meaningful return otherwise difficult to capture.

These mechanisms are first-pass. As the Hub grows, additional structures will be needed for sustained contribution, dataset quality, and community-led curation. We treat the incentive layer as something to evolve, not finalize.

5.4 A Continuum, Not a Catalog

The Hub is designed as a continuum rather than a static catalog. A catalog is a snapshot — going stale from the moment it is published. A continuum is a living system, with active contribution, curation, and growth.

This has design consequences. The ontology must be extensible — new tasks, domains, and language variants must be addable without breaking existing entries. Versioning must be first-class. The contribution path must be low-friction: a researcher who has just produced a dataset must be able to publish it in minutes, not weeks. And the social layer — the relationships among contributors, users, and curators — must be visible and supported, not buried under interface friction.

06 Open Problems

The Hub does not resolve every question implied by the dataset layer it begins to build. Naming the open ones honestly is more useful than papering over them.

6.1 Regulatory Heterogeneity

Data protection, copyright, and public-data access regimes differ country by country. A dataset assembled from public health records in one country may be uncomplicated to publish; the same effort in a neighbor may be legally fraught. Frameworks around scraped web data, government open-data mandates, indigenous-language data sovereignty, and personal-data protection do not align across borders. The Hub cannot resolve these — they are matters of national law — but it must surface the licensing and legal-jurisdiction information of every dataset it indexes, so a practitioner can decide with the relevant context visible.

6.2 Shared Standards

There are no shared standards for dataset metadata, licensing, or quality in LATAM. Two datasets nominally about “Spanish transcription” may use incompatible annotation schemas, incompatible licenses, and incompatible quality bars for what counts as a clean example. Without shared standards, datasets cannot be combined or compared. We do not propose a single standard here — that work is too large for a kickstart — but we name the gap and treat the Hub's initial metadata schema as a first proposal to be debated, not a final answer.

6.3 Collaboration Norms

Separate from metadata standards, the Hub raises governance questions not yet answered:

  • Attribution. When organization A contributes a dataset and organization B improves it (cleans it, adds annotations, fixes errors), how is credit allocated and represented?
  • Versioning. As datasets evolve, how do downstream users — including model trainers who have already trained on prior versions — track which version they are working with?
  • Arbitration. When a community contests how a dataset represents it — an indigenous community objecting to how their language has been transcribed and tagged — how is that objection processed, and what authority resolves it?
  • Review. What review process gates entry: open by default with post-hoc moderation, curated pre-publication review, or a hybrid?
  • License inheritance. When datasets are combined, how do their licenses propagate, and how is this enforced or merely tracked?

The Hub has initial answers; none should be treated as final. These are questions for the regional community to decide together.

6.4 Institutional Incentives

Universities, governments, research agencies, and public bodies across LATAM hold significant volumes of data that would be valuable as published datasets. Most never gets published, for a mix of reasons: no career incentive, no institutional mandate, no clear pathway, no budget for the cleanup required before release. The Hub's visibility mechanisms address part of this, but the deeper problem requires policy work outside the Hub: grant requirements that mandate dataset publication, university promotion criteria that value dataset contributions alongside papers, government open-data policies that treat AI-readable formats as a first-class deliverable. These are matters for the regional academic and policy communities. The Hub is a precondition for those conversations, not a substitute for them.

07 A Kickstart and an Invitation

This paper is a kickstart — a working artifact and a position, not a finished proposal. The Surus team has built the first version of the Data Hub because somebody had to begin, and the dataset layer for LATAM AI is too important to wait for. But the Hub does not belong to us, and it cannot be built by us alone.

We invite three kinds of contribution.

To researchers and institutions holding data: publish into the Hub. Whether your dataset is the appendix of a paper from three years ago, the working corpus of an active project, or the internal archive of an institution that has never published anything — the Hub is built to receive it. Every marginal contribution increases the value of every prior one.

To AI practitioners across the region: use the Hub. The most important signal that the Hub matters is that practitioners are choosing datasets from it, building models against it, and evaluating systems through it. Use is what converts a commons from a directory into infrastructure.

To the broader community — universities, foundations, governments, companies: help shape what comes next. The ontology will evolve. The standards must be debated. The collaboration norms must be defined. The benchmark layer must be built alongside the dataset layer. None of this can be the work of a single team.

The dataset layer for Latin American AI will be built by the region, for the region, or it will not be built at all. The Data Hub is one step toward that future. We invite you to take the next one with us.
